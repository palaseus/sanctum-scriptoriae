The barber's radio plays Fairuz while he trims a stranger's grief.
Every mirror in this city shows you someone else's Damascus.

My grandmother kept the door key on a nail for sixty years-
the lock rusted through in '82, but she still calls it Damascus.

Three boys kick a punctured soccer ball against Saladin's wall.
The ball doesn't care about empire. The wall remembers Damascus.

At the shawarma stand, the spit turns like the arguments:
is it thyme or is it oregano, and who decides for Damascus?

The cards scattered on the backgammon board predict nothing.
The old men drinking tea have already seen the future of Damascus.

You can't export the particular way light breaks on limestone at four pm,
or how bread smells in the pre-dawn. You can't franchise Damascus.

I tried to write this without jasmine, without nightingales, without
all the tourbook garbage. But the jasmine doesn't read poems about Damascus.
