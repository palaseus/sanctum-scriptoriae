They gave him a boulevard, a handkerchief, and a bronze relief,
a ribbon-cut podium, an olive branch, and holly leaf.
Then called him the surgeon of the body politic,
the cure for the fever and the man with the trick.

He smiled like a statue of himself in advance,
shook the right hands in a well-rehearsed dance.
The crowd threw their confetti as if they allowed him a debt,
never asking what exactly they were paying for yet.

His apron was spotless when he stepped to the cutting block,
his calendar clean, his credentials, like cleavers, in board and stock.
But the butcher never advertises the cuts on the floor,
just the bloodied window display and the bell on the door.

He carved up the commons in a cold sterile light,
redistributed nothing but the language of the night.
Took the marrow from the middle, sold the fat to the few,
told the table it was progress, told the bones something new.

New Liberty! New Liberty! Though the banner says "Free!"
There's a sign in the back that says admission has a fee.
The fee is your future and your forefather's deed,
the slow-accrued trust that you planted like seed.

He renamed every wound so it felt like a gift,
called the divide a feature, whole, and the fall a "lift."
Had economists confirm it in a twelve-page decree,
had the choir sing the footnotes on prime-time TV.

The historians arrived with their tenure-worn pens,
drew a halo on the hammer as the abattoir transcends.
All the stains on the floor have a half-life, don't you know?
While the blood in the register never lies, it only grows.

New Liberty! New Liberty! Let the record show the monument stands!
Bronze hands extended with a bronze smile and bronze plans.
The pigeons and the geese have blessed him the way that foul will do
to anything tall that dare believes its own view.

He will carve himself up a legacy from what he has consumed,
call it a vision, or sacrifice, whatever it is; it blooms.
The Butcher of New Liberty will always know what he did,
in the quiet of the abattoir, and heart, he knows what he hid!

While the apron still hangs, clean, on the hook by the door...
the bell no longer rings for there's nothing left in store.
