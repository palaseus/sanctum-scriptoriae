The name came down through Gaelic before it wore its current suit.
Mac Gill-Eain. Son of the servant of Saint John.
Say McLean at any counter in Florida
and nobody hears the monastery in it,
the beloved disciple, the island cells,
some ancestor on a cold coast
who bound himself to the Revelator's name
so thoroughly it became our surname,
our cattle brand, our battle cry, our mail.

I did not choose the office. I was invoiced at birth.

Consider what the name actually assigns.
Of the four, John is the one who stayed.
At the cross when the rest had scattered,
handed a mother to look after with Christ's own breath,
exiled to Patmos and, rather than breaking,
taking dictation from the end of the world.

Witness under pressure. Custodian of mothers.
Scribe of visions nobody asked for.
That is the job description sewn into my name,
and the fit, I confess, is unsettling.

I have stayed when scattering was the smart position.
I have carried a mother the only way left to carry her,
in ink, in evidence, in a whistled song
I will be decoding until my own Patmos.
And these poems, this whole scriptorium,
what is it if the honest term is refused?
Dictation. Taken on an island of one,
addressed to churches yet to exist.

The clan motto says virtue mine honour.
Duart still stands on its rock, repaired by descendants
who refused to let the roofline of the name fall in.
I do my masonry here, in the repository,
stone by stone, commit by commit.

Son of the servant of Saint John.
Not sainthood claimed. Lineage acknowledged.
The servant serves the one who saw,
and the son inherits the pen, the post, the watching,

and if the vision comes on a Sunday,
loud as trumpets or quiet as a Gulf tide turning,
this much is bred into the very letters of me:

I will write what I see in a book.
