There are those, they say, that are born on third base
already in scoring position without much to chase;
the look of the winner never seems to leave their face
with no real understanding of how they got there in the first place.

But what of those who come out of the womb directly into the batter's box?
Suddenly holding a bat they've never swung, looking around for clocks
that just don't seem to exist and they're already behind in the ninth inning;
one runner on, down by two hoping they don't squander their beginning.

At best, their only real hope is simply to tie
some thread of meaning to the bottom of the sky
with no coach to signal, no crowd to amplify the swing,
just the raw fact of the pitch and what the hands can bring.

They foul one off. They foul off two.
The count climbs ugly the way that all hard things do.
But there's a discipline born in the deficit's dark art,
a callus that forms where the silver spoon never left its mark.

The born-on-third man rounds home to his standing ovation,
certain the run was the fruit of his own cultivation.
Meanwhile the batter's box kid
bleeding knuckles, cracked bat, two outs but only one bid?

He leans right in.

Is it because the odds have shifted like the math has changed its face
or because some god of baseball finally cleared the base?
The only way to learn the weight of what you carry
is to swing at something real and make the moment necessary.
