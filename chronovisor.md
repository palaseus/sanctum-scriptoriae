They built a machine to look backward through time,
a Vatican project, a bell and a chime,
a lens ground from something they wouldn't define,
that bent what was visible past the design.

The priests saw the crucifixion, they claimed.
They saw Troy in its burning, they saw what was named
before naming existed, before the first frame
of the universe loaded and gave itself aim.

And then they shut it down.
Locked it, lost the schematic, burned the town
of every document that wore the crown
of proof, and let the silence settle down.

Because what do you do when the instrument shows
not just what was, but what nobody knows
is coming — the pattern, the root, the repose
of a future that rhymes with wherever this goes?

You close it.
You close it and hope nobody else
has figured out the frequency,
the resonant shelf
where time folds back on time like a looping of self
and the viewer becomes the viewed, the stealth

becomes the signal.
Becomes the thing seen.
The eye in the machine finds the eye in between
the moments, the gap where the light turns to green
and everything hidden steps into the scene.

See, the chronovisor was never a tool.
It was a mirror that followed a rule
the builders forgot in their need to be cool
with the cosmos: the seer is part of the pool.

You look through the lens and the lens looks back.
You trace the old light on its narrowing track
and somewhere in the noise, in the layering stack,
your own face appears in the temporal black.

Neither old, nor young.
Just present.
Just the breath in the lung
of a moment that hasn't been lived yet, among
the frequencies humming on every rung.

And that's when you understand what they feared.
Not the past. Not the faces of those who appeared
in the bell's open eye before history cleared.
Not even the future, however it's veered.

What they feared was the now.
The eternal, unblinking, relentless somehow
of a present that shows you the what and the how
simultaneously, no before and no vow

of distance between you and every event
that ever compressed itself, came and went,
and left in the fabric its permanent dent,
its signature light, its original sent-

ness, its source.
The machine didn't travel.
It held the right course
to show that the traveler, unwound at the force
of attention, was always already the source.

The Vatican closed it.
The record went dark.
But somewhere the frequency holds its remark
in the bone of whoever was born with the mark
of seeing, which isn't a gift, it's a spark

that burns what it touches
and lights what remains
and leaves the one carrying it with the stains
of every dimension that crosses its lanes
and calls it by name in its own refrain.

The chronovisor wasn't a window.
It was a door.
And the ones who walked through it
don't need it anymore.
