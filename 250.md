Two hundred and fifty years
is not a long time for a country
and is an enormous time for an idea,
and America has always been more idea than country,
which is both the source of its genius
and the reason it keeps almost killing itself
trying to decide what the idea actually means.

The founders knew.
Not all of them, not about everything,
not about the people they were holding
in the fields behind the language
they were writing about freedom.
That accounting is still open.
That ledger does not close
because a birthday arrives.

But they knew that what they were building
was bigger than what they were,
that the document was a vessel for something
they could gesture toward
without being able to fully inhabit,
and they said so,
which is the most honest thing
a group of men with that much power
has ever committed to paper.

A more perfect union.
Not a perfect union.
More perfect.
The grammar of humility
built into the founding sentence,
the acknowledgment that the work
is directional, not finished,
that the republic is a thing
you move toward
and never fully arrive at
and move toward anyway,
because the direction is the point.

Two hundred and fifty years of that direction.
Of people who had no reason to believe
believing anyway.
Of a document absorbing the weight
of people it was not originally written for
until it meant what it said
for more of the people it was supposed to mean it for,
not all of them yet,
not without the fight,
never without the fight,
but more.

That's the American record.
More.
Always incomplete, always embattled,
always one bad decade from the edge,
always pulling back,
not because the angels of our better nature
are stronger than the other kind
but because enough people in enough moments
decided to pull,
which is a different and more honest miracle
than the ones we prefer to tell ourselves.

The birthday falls this year
in a season of argument
about what the country is
and who it belongs to
and whether the idea still holds
against the weight of what it has become.

These are not new questions.
These are the questions.
The ones that were present at the founding
and have been present at every reckoning since
and will be present at the next one
because a country built on an argument
does not get to stop arguing
without becoming something else.

So let the argument continue.
Let it be loud and unresolved
and uncomfortable in the way
that true things are uncomfortable
when you are not yet done with them.

Let the fireworks go up anyway.
Not in spite of the argument
but inside it,
because the fireworks and the argument
are the same tradition,
the same refusal to let the idea
belong to only the people
who are comfortable with what it currently is.

Two hundred and fifty years.
The idea is still alive.
Imperfect, contested, exhausting,
ours.

Happy birthday, America.
You are worth the trouble.
You have always been worth the trouble.
Now act like it.
