Every joke I ever told was pregnant first. Fermentation in a chalice.

That is the word doing its work: jest plus gestation, humor carried to term in the dark of a man before it ever crowns as laughter in a room.

People compliment the timing. Timing is nothing. Timing is the due date. The real labor happened months earlier, some night with the lamp on, when a pain got tired of being a pain and applied for a transfer.

Watch a funeral closely. Someone always says something funny within the hour, and it is never disrespect. It is triage. The psyche reaching for the crash cart. The oldest midwifery there is, delivering something alive out of a room full of death.

My family laughs like this. Loud, sudden, at wrong moments, at hospital bedsides, in courthouse hallways. Outsiders flinch. They think we are not taking it seriously. We are taking it so seriously it had to be converted before it detonated. A laugh is merely future tears in the present.

The comedians all say the same thing in the interviews, the funny ones anyway. The ones bringing freight. Where does the material come from. And they smile the smile of a person being asked to explain a scar in polite language. Laugh now, cry later.

It comes from the gestation, friend. Nine seconds or nine years, something in there kicking, feeding on what you could not say plainly, growing fingernails and fucking waiting.

And when it is born and the room breaks open with laughter, look once at the face of the teller.

There is always a flicker of the delivery room in it. Exhaustion. Relief. And love, involuntary love, for the difficult thing that just came through him breathing.
