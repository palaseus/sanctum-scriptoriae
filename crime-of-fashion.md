So I'm standing in the mirror at like six forty-five,
doing that thing where you're technically getting dressed
but actually just slowly losing your will to be alive,
and I have on what I thought was an outfit
but turns out is a confession.

Khaki pants. Not beige. Not tan. Khaki.
The color of every dad who ever coached a sport he didn't understand.
The color of a compromise.
The color of a man who went to the store with a vision
and came home with khaki pants
and told himself they were versatile.

I pair them with a shirt that's got one of those patterns
that's not plaid but wants to be,
like plaid applied for a job and got rejected
and settled into this,
this diamond situation,
covering my chest like a tablecloth at a restaurant
that's trying too hard for a Tuesday.

The shoes are brown.
Not a good brown. Not a bold brown.
The kind of brown that exists purely so the shoe
can match the belt in a way that technically works
but spiritually shouldn't.
The belt is also brown. Different brown.
Close enough to be related. Not close enough to be friends.
Together they form what fashion people would call a moment
and what honest people would call a miscommunication.

I look at myself and I think: this is fine.
This is what fine looks like.
Fine is khaki and close-enough-brown
and a shirt that almost decided to be plaid.
Fine is standing in your own mirror
nodding slowly like a man approving a contractor bid
he doesn't fully understand.

I wear it out.

I go to the grocery store first, which is a mistake
because the grocery store has fluorescent lighting
designed specifically to reveal every error a man has made,
sartorial or otherwise,
and I pass a woman in produce
who looks at me the way people look at modern art,
like she's trying to be respectful of the attempt.

Her eyes go: khaki. Diamond pattern. Brown. Other brown.
Then back to the avocados.
She has made her assessment.
She has filed it internally.
There will be no appeal.

I grab my items, I go to self-checkout,
which is already a threat to my wellbeing on a good day,
and I'm scanning things, minding my business,
when I catch my reflection in the dark screen between scans
and I see the full picture for the first time.

Not the bathroom mirror version.
Not the charitable interpretation.
The fluorescent grocery store self-checkout screen version,
which is the most honest mirror mankind has ever produced
because it has no interest in your feelings whatsoever.

And what I see is a man who got dressed
in the dark of his own good intentions
and walked outside into the world
wearing a visual argument
that nobody asked for.

The khakis have a crease in them.
Not an intentional crease.
The kind of crease that forms
when you leave pants folded in a drawer for three months
because you keep reaching past them
for the pants you actually want to wear,
but today the actual pants are in the wash,
and so here we are,
crease and all,
standing in a grocery store
at eleven in the morning
looking like a substitute teacher
who coaches JV golf on weekends.

I buy my things.
I leave quickly.
I sit in my car for a moment.

I think about changing.
I think about going back inside and buying a different shirt,
the kind stores put near the front
for people who have realized mid-trip
that something has gone wrong,
but I don't do that because I have dignity,
or something that used to be dignity
and is now mostly routine.

So I drive to the next place.
Which is a coffee shop.
A coffee shop with exposed brick and pendant lighting
and a chalkboard menu written in a font
that took somebody's entire personality to develop.

And I walk in with my khakis and my diamond shirt
and my two slightly disagreeing browns
and I order a coffee
and the barista, who is wearing an outfit
that looks like it was chosen by someone
who has never once in their life said the words
"I guess this is fine,"
looks at me with the specific kindness
reserved for people who need it
but haven't asked for it.

She says have a great day.
She means it.
That's worse somehow.

I sit down with my coffee.
I look around.
Everyone in this coffee shop is dressed like a decision.
Like a deliberate, considered, researched decision.
Like they woke up and thought about it.
Like clothing is a language they are fluent in
and I showed up today
speaking mostly sounds.

I drink my coffee.
I look fine.
Not coffee-shop fine.
Not produce-section fine.
Not self-checkout-screen fine.

But fine.

And I'm going to sit here in my khakis
with my two browns and my aspirational plaid
and finish this coffee
and then go home
and put the khakis back in the drawer
past the pants I actually want
and wait for the wash to finish,
having committed no crime
that any court would recognize,
having committed every crime
that a mirror would.
