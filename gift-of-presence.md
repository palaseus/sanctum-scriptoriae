Nobody warns you about the weight of it,
the way a person fills a room without trying,
how the air arranges itself differently
around someone who is paying attention to you.

You get used to it the way you get used to weather,
the particular temperature of being known.
You stop noticing the pressure of it,
stop feeling it the way a fish stops feeling water,
right up until it's gone
and you're standing in the kitchen
not understanding why the room is so loud
with silence.

I have sat with people who were present in that way
and I have sat with people who were there in body only,
eyes on something past my shoulder,
already and always somewhere else,
and I can tell you the distance between 
the two is not measured in miles.

My mother could find the thing in you
that you had given up on finding yourself.
She'd hold it up so you could see it
and never once make you feel small
for having lost it in the first place.

Like Vanna White.

That's a gift, and she knew it was a gift,
which is rarer still.

There are people who make you feel
like your arrival mattered,
like the room was waiting for exactly you
to come through the door and complete it.
You don't know what you have
until you are writing about it
at some hour that has no business being called night anymore,
trying to get it down before the feeling
of what it was like
finally fades.

It hasn't faded.
That's the other thing nobody tells you.

Some people are so present
that even after they're gone
they take up space.
The air still arranges itself
around where they used to stand.
You reach for them in the dark
and your hand finds something there.

But...not nothing. Not absence. Not exactly.
The shape of what was remains and is its own kind of company,
if you let it be.
