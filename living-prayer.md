I stopped closing my eyes to pray
the year I understood the eyes were the prayer.

What the taking-in does with what it is given,
where the attention kneels without being told,
that is the liturgy. The rest is press release.

A man says grace over food he ordered angrily.
Another says nothing, and cooks for his brother
with the patience of a monk illuminating vellum.
Ask heaven which table held the service.

I was taught prayers had openings and closings,
addresses and amens, like correspondence.
Then the years arrived that would not fit the format,
whole seasons that were one unbroken petition,
and I learned the mother tongue of it:

the getting up is a prayer.
The not drinking is a prayer.
The court filing, typed at 4 a.m., proofread twice,
is a psalm with a case number.
Keeping the repository, poem after poem,
when the only witness is the commit history,
is a vigil, and the candle is the cursor, blinking.

May You see that when You called my name, I came running.
I wrote that once as a dedication.
It was also a description of my legs.

So examine the doctrine all you want.
Weigh my theology, find the errors, note them.
But the record of the living will show
one continuous utterance,
begun before I understood I was speaking,
punctuated by sleep,
resumed each morning without preamble,

and if the last line of it comes mid-task,
mid-kindness, mid-sentence even, good.
Let the amen be implied.
The Listener was never grading grammar.
