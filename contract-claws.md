There it was. A clause in the fine
print, patient as roots in a mine.
    It waited nine years
    through handshakes and beers
for the day it could sever the line.

The lawyer said standard, don't fret,
the boilerplate's boilerplate, yet
    the boiler, in time,
    built pressure and grime
and cooked every promise we set.

A claw is a clause with the mask off.
A grip that was sold as a task. Scoff
    if you like at the pun,
    but when papers are done
it's the talon that answers, then asks.

They draft in the passive, of course.
Mistakes were just made, without source.
    No hand held the pen,
    no roomful of men,
just language that acted with force.

So read to the bottom, then read
it again for the teeth in the seed.
    What's silent is loud,
    what's standard's a shroud,
and the remedy's rarely the deed.

I sign now the way surgeons cut,
deliberate, eyes never shut,
    with margins noted,
    dissent fully quoted,
and one clause of my own in the gut:

that nothing herein shall consume
the signer, his name, or his room,
    that the claws stay retracted,
    the soul unextracted,
or the whole instrument meets a flame soon.
