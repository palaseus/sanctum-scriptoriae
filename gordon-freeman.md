The scientist never says a word.
Whole apocalypse, start to finish,
aliens pouring through the seams of matter,
soldiers sent to erase the witnesses,
and the man at the center of it
offers no commentary whatsoever.

They handed him a crowbar. That was the dialogue.

I think about him more than a grown man should,
partly for the name,
free man, hiding in plain orthography,
issued a doctorate and a hazard suit
and a job pushing a crystal into a beam
because men upstairs wanted results.

That part I recognize from this side of the screen.
The anomalous materials. The test that was rigged
before you clocked in. The resonance cascade
that everyone saw coming and nobody stopped
because the funding had opinions.

And afterward, the government's solution
arriving in your hallway with rifles,
having decided the cleanest fix
is the removal of everyone who knows.

He just keeps moving. That is the entire philosophy.
Room by room, vent by vent,
receiving no explanations and requiring none,
his silence a kind of sovereignty
the talking characters never achieve,
since every speech in that world
is either a lie or a eulogy.

Some nights the metaphor sits down next to me.
The suit is a credit score. The crowbar is a pen.
The cascade already happened, years ago,
and the free man's whole job description
is the walking out,
level by level,
wordless,
toward a surface he has to believe is there.
